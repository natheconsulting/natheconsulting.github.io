# When the status report still shows green – and everyone in the room knows it shouldn't.

There is a moment in every critical IT project that everyone recognises and no one says out loud.

The steering committee meets. The report shows amber. It should be red. The business side and IT haven't been speaking the same language for weeks. The budget is under pressure. And the original goal – the actual value this project was supposed to deliver – has been lost somewhere between sprint reviews and escalation emails.

In that moment, you don't need more process. You need someone who has been there before.

Most IT projects don't fail because of the technology. They fail because at some point, no one is saying out loud: "What is this actually delivering for the business?" Stop asking that question, and you ship features. Stop hearing it, and you burn budget.

---

## When your project needs an experienced hand

Before I became an independent consultant, I was a manager and managing director at IT consultancies. During that time, I built a branch office from two people to over 70 – with full responsibility for growth, headcount, sales, and financial results.

That wasn't project responsibility. That was entrepreneurial responsibility.

Since founding Nathe Consulting in 2024, I bring exactly that experience to my interim mandates as a project manager – in three areas that determine whether a project succeeds:

---

### Entrepreneurial thinking

A transformation programme with an eight-year timeline doesn't have one project sponsor – it has several, one after another. At a certain point, the initiative had no active management backing. The choice in that moment: keep going, make the vacuum visible, and fill it yourself – or wait for the next leadership layer and risk the project going quiet.

I kept going. The programme was delivered on time.

Entrepreneurial thinking doesn't mean avoiding risks. It means naming them, assessing them – and then taking responsibility.

---

### Bridging business and IT

On a machine learning project, the business side asked about project progress. The correct answer wasn't "Sprint 7 is complete" – it was the F1 score of the model. Because real progress in an ML project isn't measured in backlog items completed, but in the quality of the model's outputs. Making that connection – between what the business needs and what the technology actually delivers – was the real project management task.

If you only speak one side's language, you can't translate. I speak both.

---

### Complex delivery structures

A legally mandated initiative, deadline non-negotiable. On the delivery side: internal IT, a prime contractor, several subcontractors on different contract types, external partner organisations with their own priorities. When a critical interface wasn't delivered as agreed, that wasn't a technical problem – it was a coordination problem across three contract layers.

Complex delivery structures need someone who can hold all levels in view simultaneously: contract, quality, timeline – and the escalation paths in between.
